## 1. Dunford's Positioning Methodology

April Dunford's **Obviously Awesome** methodology is the de facto standard for B2B SaaS positioning. The process:

**Step 1: Identify real competitive alternatives**
- What were successful customers using before? Include "do nothing," spreadsheets, manual processes, point tools.
- Base this on actual customer data, not market maps.

**Step 2: Catalog unique attributes**
- Features, architecture, integrations, data assets, approach — anything the product has that alternatives don't.
- Be specific and concrete, not aspirational.

**Step 3: Map attributes to value themes**
- Group attributes into clusters that deliver meaningful outcomes.
- Each cluster = one value theme (e.g., "faster implementation," "more reliable data").

**Step 4: Determine best-fit customers**
- Who cares most about those value themes?
- Define by role, company characteristics, and situation/trigger — not demographics.

**Step 5: Select market category**
- Category sets buyer expectations. Pick one that makes your strengths obvious.
- If an existing category creates the right context, use it. Don't invent a new one unless necessary.
- Warning: if the category's default assumptions conflict with what you deliver, the category will override your messaging.

**Step 6: Positioning statement and narrative**
- Flows from the above. Feed into sales stories, website, campaigns.

**Key Dunford principles:**
- Start from customers who are already succeeding (post-PMF data mining).
- Positioning is NOT copywriting — it's the strategic decision that constrains all messaging.
- When companies treat positioning as a marketing copy exercise, they avoid hard trade-offs.
- Most companies should win within an existing category frame before attempting category creation.

## 2. Ries & Trout: Classic Positioning

Core concept: Positioning is "an organized system for finding a way into the mind." It adjusts to what already exists in the customer's mental map.

**Key principles:**
- Positioning is fundamentally **comparative** — it's the relative place a product occupies vs. alternatives in the same category.
- In crowded markets, position as leader or specialist within a well-understood category rather than inventing a new mental slot.
- Claim a simple, defensible idea ("the leading X," "the first Y," "the [attribute] choice") and stick with it.
- Early-stage SaaS: pick a subsegment within a known category ("CRM for construction subcontractors") and own it before expanding.

**Still relevant for B2B SaaS because:**
- Many categories (CRM, ERP, data warehouse, marketing automation) have strong existing mental associations.
- Trying to create a net-new category may waste resources and confuse buyers.

## 3. Category Design (Lochhead / Play Bigger)

Christopher Lochhead's discipline of creating and dominating new market categories.

**When to use category design:**
- The product introduces a genuinely new problem-solution space.
- Existing categories cannot contain what you do without creating confusion.
- You have capital and time for market education.

**Category design involves:**
- Naming and framing a new problem
- Defining the new category that solves it
- Designing product, company, and market education in concert
- Category designers don't sell better products — they introduce something *different*

**Sangram Vajre's movement-building variant (#FlipMyFunnel):**
- Reframe an existing practice (lead-based marketing → account-based marketing)
- Name a community around the new frame
- Build the category through the movement

**Decision rule:**
Default to category choice (Dunford/Ries & Trout) unless there is strong evidence of a genuinely new problem-solution pairing. Most companies should not attempt category creation.

## 4. Raskin's Strategic Narrative

Andy Raskin's 5-part narrative structure (from the Zuora "Greatest Sales Deck" analysis):

1. **Name the big, relevant change in the world** — An undeniable shift (e.g., subscription economy, AI transformation)
2. **Show there will be winners and losers** — Stakes based on how they respond to the change
3. **Introduce the "promised land"** — The desired future state for the audience
4. **Show the obstacles** — What makes reaching the promised land hard
5. **Position your product as the vehicle** — How it helps the customer (hero) reach the promised land

**Key principles:**
- Start from the world, not from the product
- The customer is the hero, the product is the vehicle
- Internal alignment on the narrative (CEO to SDR) is essential
- This narrative lives in vision pitch decks, website hero sections, executive keynotes, then cascades into sales decks and campaigns

## 5. Moesta's JTBD & Demand-Side Sales

Bob Moesta's Jobs-to-be-Done applied to positioning and sales.

**Core concept:** Customers "hire" a product to make progress on a job within a specific context.

**Four forces of progress:**
1. **Push** — Dissatisfaction with current situation
2. **Pull** — Attraction of a new solution
3. **Anxiety** — Fear about the new solution (will it work? migration risk?)
4. **Habit** — Comfort with the current way of doing things

For a switch to happen: Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit

**JTBD Timeline:**
First thought → Passive looking → Active looking → Deciding → Onboarding → Ongoing use

**Hiring criteria (three dimensions):**
- Functional: Does it do the job?
- Emotional: How does it make me feel?
- Social: How do others perceive my choice?

**Implications for positioning:**
- Frame products in terms of progress: "When X happens, this helps you get to Y without Z downside"
- Primary competitor is the customer's current way of making progress (patchwork of tools + habits), not just other vendors
- Sales should act as "concierges" helping customers make progress, not feature-pushers
- Onboarding messaging should reiterate the core job, celebrate early wins, and address anxieties

## 6. Laja's Message-Market Fit

Peep Laja's 4-point framework for evaluating messaging:

1. **Clarity** — Do people immediately understand what the product is and does?
2. **Relevance** — Can the right audience recognize it is for them?
3. **Value** — Is the value compelling enough to care?
4. **Differentiation** — Is it clear how this is different or better than alternatives?

**Laja's differentiation principles:**
- Differentiation should be "deep in the product" (unique data, algorithms, integrations) — not trivial UI variations.
- Avoid vague, undifferentiated claims ("increase revenue," "be more efficient").
- State value in specific, concrete terms tied to unique capabilities.
- Messaging should "say something interesting" and contrast clearly with competitor promises.
- Explicitly name competitors and the status quo, then contrast meaningful capabilities and outcomes.

**Validation methods:**
- Panel-based research (e.g., Wynter): target B2B buyers review messaging and give structured feedback
- A/B tests on key surfaces (hero headlines, CTAs)
- Audience research tools (SparkToro) for language and channel insight
- Win-loss analysis and JTBD interviews

## 7. Pierri's Homepage Positioning

Anthony Pierri / FletchPMM's homepage-centric positioning approach.

**Core belief:** "Your homepage IS your positioning" — it's the shared artifact every stakeholder sees.

**Homepage messaging order:**
1. Lead with a clear "what it is" statement (category + capability)
2. Immediately clarify "who it's for" (champion role + segment)
3. Show concrete use cases and workflows ("when you'd use it")
4. Connect to outcomes and social proof

**For novel workflows (contextual differentiation):**
- The buyer's question isn't "why you vs. competitor?" — it's "when would I use this at all?"
- Positioning must describe specific use cases and triggers, not feature comparisons
- Add a "capability" layer between features and benefits: describe concrete actions users can take

**Pierri's test:** Can a stranger understand the homepage in a few seconds? If not, it's a positioning problem, not a design problem.

## 8. Stratton's Messaging Altitude

Emma Stratton's approach to tailoring messaging by audience altitude:

**Executive altitude:** Big-picture outcomes, risk reduction, strategic alignment
**User/champion altitude:** Concrete workflow improvements, relief from daily frustrations

**Stratton's principles:**
- Good messaging feels human and simple, even when the technology is complex
- The job of messaging is to "simplify strategy" into words people actually use
- Many teams ask for "punchier messaging" when the real issue is muddy strategy
- If teams can't answer "who is this for?" and "what do they get?" in simple language, no amount of editing will rescue the messaging

## 9. Differentiation Models

Four recurring lenses (synthesized from multiple practitioners):

### Capability differentiation
*What the product can uniquely do*
- Unique data, algorithms, integrations, architecture
- Describe concrete actions users can take (capability layer between features and outcomes)

### Workflow differentiation
*How work gets done differently*
- New workflow introduced by the product (e.g., scheduling via link instead of back-and-forth)
- Changing when and how customers perform steps can be more differentiating than any single feature

### Outcome differentiation
*What results customers achieve that they couldn't before*
- Frame in customer language: "close deals 30% faster with fewer no-decisions"
- Each cluster of attributes should roll up to a differentiated outcome

### Worldview differentiation
*How the company's beliefs about the future differ*
- Raskin's "change in the world" narratives
- Category design and movement-building (Lochhead, Vajre)
- Challenges the status quo with a strong point of view

**Best practice:** Combine at least two lenses. E.g., unique capabilities → new workflow → measurable outcomes, aligned with an emergent worldview.

## 10. Competitive Framing Rules

1. Always include status quo and "do nothing" as explicit alternatives
2. Define alternatives based on what customers would actually do if your product didn't exist
3. For mature categories: clarify *how* you win in specific situations (implementation speed, data model, workflow fit) — not broad claims
4. For new workflows: educate *when and why* to use the product, anchoring to concrete job contexts
5. Avoid superficial differentiation claims ("better UX," "more features") — state deep product-level differences
6. Name competitors explicitly when contrasting meaningful capabilities and outcomes

## 11. Messaging Architecture Template

### Level 1: Strategic Narrative
One-paragraph story: change in the world → stakes → promised land → obstacles → product as vehicle

### Level 2: Core Value Propositions (3-5)
For each:
- **Claim:** What outcome the customer gets
- **Capability:** What they can concretely do (the action layer)
- **Proof:** Data, customer story, or third-party validation

### Level 3: Persona Messages
| Persona | Altitude | Focus |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Economic buyer (VP/C-suite) | High | ROI, risk reduction, strategic alignment |
| Champion (day-to-day buyer) | Medium | Workflow improvement, team impact, career win |
| End user | Ground | Daily relief, ease of use, time saved |

### Level 4: Channel Adaptation
- **Website:** Pierri's framework — what → who → when → outcomes → proof
- **Sales conversations:** JTBD discovery → forces exploration → progress framing
- **Outbound:** Orlob-style — describe the buyer's problem better than they can
- **Product onboarding:** Reiterate job, celebrate wins, address anxiety
- **Content/campaigns:** Kramer's big-bet themes tied to positioning

**Handoff note:** This skill produces Levels 1-3. For Level 4 execution, hand off to the relevant downstream skill (copywriter, sales, prospecting, etc.).

## 12. Common Failure Modes

When auditing, check for these patterns:

1. **No single source of truth** — Positioning discussed verbally or in scattered slides, no canonical document
2. **Lack of executive ownership** — Positioning treated as a marketing task; conflicting strategic decisions pull the company in multiple directions
3. **No integration with GTM** — Positioning project ends without updating ICP definitions, campaigns, or sales enablement
4. **Website lags strategy** — "Don't look at our website" after a positioning project = the work isn't operationalized
5. **No feedback loop** — No way to assess if new messaging improved win rates, conversion, or recall
6. **Feature-first framing** — Messaging leads with what the product does instead of what progress customers make
7. **Trying to be everything** — Refusing to pick a narrow ICP or commit to a category
8. **Copywriting before strategy** — Jumping to "wordsmithing" without resolving who it's for, what it replaces, and why it wins

## 13. Implementation Roadmap

For mid-market B2B SaaS (general industry practice):

### Phase 1: Discovery (4-6 weeks)
- Analyze best-fit customers and recent wins
- Conduct 10-20 JTBD-style interviews
- Map competitive alternatives including status quo
- Run audience research (media habits, language, channels)

### Phase 2: Positioning Decisions (2-4 weeks)
- Cross-functional workshop: ICP, competitive set, attributes, value themes, category
- Draft positioning document
- Draft strategic narrative (Raskin structure)
- Validate with subset of customers/prospects

### Phase 3: Messaging Architecture (2-4 weeks)
- Translate positioning into value props, proof points, messaging hierarchy
- Build persona- and channel-level messaging matrix
- Test key messages with target buyers (panel research / Wynter-style)

### Phase 4: Execution Planning (2-4 weeks)
- Update website (Pierri-style homepage framework)
- Create sales decks, battlecards, talk tracks
- Define 2-3 integrated campaign themes (Kramer's big-bet approach)

### Phase 5: Rollout & Enablement (ongoing)
- Internal training on positioning and narrative
- Instrument metrics (conversion, win rate, cycle time, activation)
- Maintain research and messaging repository
- Update positioning as audience, category, and product evolve
